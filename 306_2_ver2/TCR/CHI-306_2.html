Background

In the context of T-cell receptors (TCRs), a “clone” refers to a group of T cells that all have the same TCR sequence. Each TCR consists of two chains: the alpha (TRA) chain and the beta (TRB) chain. These chains come together to form a functional TCR that can recognize specific antigens.

Here’s a simple explanation of TRA and TRB chains:

TRA Chain: The alpha chain of the TCR is one of the two chains that make up the TCR complex. It contains variable (V), diversity (D), and joining (J) gene segments, which recombine to generate a diverse repertoire of TCR sequences. The TRA chain contributes to the specificity of the TCR for antigen recognition.

TRB Chain: The beta chain of the TCR is the other chain that makes up the TCR complex. Similar to the TRA chain, it also contains variable (V), diversity (D), and joining (J) gene segments, allowing for diversity in TCR sequences. The TRB chain plays a crucial role in antigen recognition and T cell activation.

In a TCR clone, both the TRA and TRB chains are paired together to form a functional TCR. Each clone consists of a unique combination of TRA and TRB sequences, which determine the antigen specificity of the T cells within that clone.

Visualizing the TRA and TRB chains in a TCR clone would involve representing the specific amino acid or nucleotide sequences of both chains that make up the TCR. This representation would illustrate how the TRA and TRB chains come together to form a unique TCR sequence within a clone.

Alignments with the COVID-specific TCR database

Glyph2 is a computational tool used in immunology research to analyze and interpret T-cell receptor (TCR) sequences. It’s specifically designed to help researchers understand how T cells respond to infections, including viruses like SARS-CoV-2, which causes COVID-19.

Here’s a simple explanation of what Glyph2 does and how it works:

ImmuneCODE Database: Glyph2 uses a database called ImmuneCODE, which contains a vast collection of TCR sequences from people who have been exposed to or infected with certain pathogens, including SARS-CoV-2. This database helps researchers compare their TCR sequences to those of known infections.

Mapping TCRs: Researchers can map their own TCR sequences to Glyph2. The tool then compares these sequences against the TCR sequences in the ImmuneCODE database. It looks for similarities or matches between the TCRs in the database and the ones from the samples.

Identification of COVID-Specific TCRs: If Glyph2 finds matches between the uploaded TCR sequences and those in the ImmuneCODE database, it suggests that the T cells in the samples may have been exposed to the virus associated with those TCR sequences. This can help researchers identify T cells that are specifically targeting the virus, which is crucial for understanding the immune response to COVID-19.

In summary, Glyph2 is a powerful tool that helps researchers analyze TCR sequences and identify T cells that are responding to specific infections, including SARS-CoV-2. By comparing TCR sequences from samples to those in the ImmuneCODE database, researchers can gain insights into the immune response to COVID-19
